# The Sovereign AI Company — Pre-Writing Kit

*Positioning artefacts, pitch language, and interview strategy. Produce
these before Chapter 1 is written. They are the tightest expression of
what the book is and who it's for — getting them right now means every
chapter you write afterwards has a touchstone to return to.*

---

## 1. Back-cover blurb

*The 150-word expression of the book. If this doesn't sound compelling,
the book hasn't fully resolved yet. Use it on the back cover, the
landing page, Amazon copy, and anywhere the book needs to sell itself
in thirty seconds.*

### Primary version (140 words)

> AI is critical infrastructure, and Europe has been renting it from
> Silicon Valley at Silicon Valley margins.
>
> The post-1945 order that made this safe is failing. Tariffs, tiered
> access to frontier models, and the CLOUD Act have turned every AI
> supply chain into a geopolitical one. Every frontier model in
> production today is American or Chinese. Mistral is the outlier, not
> the exception.
>
> This book is not a call to stop using OpenAI or Anthropic. It is a
> case for the Plan B that keeps European companies running if access
> is cut, the honest playbook for hedging across compute, models, data,
> and talent, and the argument for the open, consortium-governed model
> ecosystem that must exist before the next shock arrives.
>
> We depend on each other. That's the starting position — not the
> conclusion.

### Shorter version (75 words — for social, ads, email signatures)

> Every frontier AI model in production today is American or Chinese.
> The post-1945 order that made this safe for European companies is
> failing. *The Sovereign AI Company* is the operational playbook for
> hedging across compute, models, data, and talent — not to abandon
> American infrastructure, but to build the Plan B that keeps European
> businesses running if access is cut, tariffed, or quietly tiered
> away. Continuity planning for the new world order.

### Editorial notes

- "Silicon Valley margins" is the sharpest line — consider putting it
  in the opening sentence of the flap copy, the first tweet, and the
  first slide of any pitch deck.
- Avoid "sovereignty" in the first sentence — it's loaded and sends
  policy readers running. Lead with "critical infrastructure" and
  "Plan B."
- "Mistral is the outlier, not the exception" is a reframe that
  rewards a second read — earns its place.
- Close with the "we depend on each other" line. It defuses the
  anti-American read that every reviewer will otherwise project onto
  the book.

## 2. Tagline candidates

*One line, on the cover or under the subtitle. Test these against the
people you'll be pitching to — friends in the target audience, not
publishers.*

**Primary (recommended)**

> *The Plan B for the post-American AI era.*

**Alternatives**

1. *AI is critical infrastructure. Act accordingly.*
2. *Why European companies need a second source for their intelligence.*
3. *Build the Plan B — before you need it.*
4. *Continuity planning for the new world order.*

The primary wins because it's short, memorable, slightly edgy without
being anti-American, and names the reader's situation clearly. The
others are stronger in specific contexts — (3) for ads, (4) for
conference decks, (1) and (2) are more explanatory.

## 3. Elevator pitches

### 30-second version (for a conference, an investor, a cold introduction)

> Every frontier AI model in production is American or Chinese. For
> European companies, that's gone from a commercial choice to a
> geopolitical dependency — tariffs, the CLOUD Act, tiered model
> access. *The Sovereign AI Company* is the operational playbook for
> hedging across compute, models, data, and talent. It's not anti-
> American. It's continuity planning for executives who can't afford
> to have their AI infrastructure cut off.

### Two-minute version (for a podcast host, an agent, a serious listener)

> The thesis is that AI has become load-bearing infrastructure — your
> company cannot actually function without it anymore, even in the
> shadow economy where employees are using ChatGPT without official
> sanction. And it's become infrastructure at the same moment the
> post-1945 rules-based order that made American cloud and American
> models safe for European companies to depend on is breaking down.
> Tariffs, tiered access, the CLOUD Act, Schrems II — every supply
> chain for AI is now a geopolitical one.
>
> The book's not saying stop using Anthropic or OpenAI. That would be
> naïve. It's saying have a Plan B that can keep your lights on if
> access is cut, and start building it now. Most EU enterprise
> workloads don't need hyperscale — European providers like Hetzner
> can handle them at a fraction of the cost. Most workloads don't
> need frontier reasoning either — Mistral and open-weight models are
> already good enough for document extraction, RAG, summarisation,
> structured generation. So the Plan B is cheaper than most executives
> think.
>
> The constructive argument is that Europe needs its own open-weight,
> consortium-governed model ecosystem — a Linux of AI — built by the
> coalition of ASML, SAP, Siemens, Mistral, defence primes, and the
> national labs that's already forming. Not autarky. A third pole,
> alongside the US and China.

## 4. Comparable titles

*For an agent pitch, publisher deck, or Amazon BSR positioning. A
comparable title is not a book that's the same as yours — it's a book
whose readers will buy yours. Aim for 3–5 recent, successful, and
genuinely similar in audience.*

### Tight comps (strongly recommended)

- **Chip War** by Chris Miller (2022). Geopolitics of semiconductors.
  Same audience: executives, policy readers, serious tech generalists.
  Sold strongly; won FT Business Book of the Year. *The Sovereign AI
  Company* is the AI-stack equivalent of what Miller did for silicon.
- **The Coming Wave** by Mustafa Suleyman (2023). AI policy and
  containment. Similar reader profile; different argument. Your book
  is the operational response where Suleyman's is the warning.
- **Power and Progress** by Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson (2023).
  Tech and political economy. Adjacent audience — readers who want
  the political frame around tech strategy.

### Adjacent comps (worth mentioning)

- **Supremacy** by Parmy Olson (2024). OpenAI vs. DeepMind. Recent,
  tech-business-reader audience.
- **AI Superpowers** by Kai-Fu Lee (2018). US-China AI framing.
  Dated but foundational — almost every AI-and-geopolitics book cites
  it.

### Positioning line

> "For readers of *Chip War* and *The Coming Wave*. The first book to
> apply the geopolitics-of-technology frame to the AI stack from a
> European executive's desk."

## 6. Opening sentence candidates

*Four working options for the first sentence of Chapter 1. Each sets a
different tone. Pick one, or let them inform your own. The opening
sentence is the reader's first impression of voice — worth the
deliberation.*

### Candidate A — Declarative, factual, confident

> "Every frontier AI model in production today is American or
> Chinese."

Sets up Part I by putting the single most important geopolitical fact
on the table immediately. Short. Ages well. Hard to argue with. The
rest of the chapter spends itself explaining why this matters, which
is exactly the structure you want.

### Candidate B — Aphoristic, sharp, present-tense

> "The European company that cannot function without ChatGPT, Claude,
> and Azure has not noticed it is running on foreign infrastructure."

Captures the book's voice — slightly contrarian, operationally
specific, slightly mocking without being mean. Good if you want a
voice-forward opening.

### Candidate C — Conversational, premise-setting

> "This book begins with a question no European board was asking
> three years ago and none can avoid today: what happens to us if
> the Americans cut us off?"

Most explicit setup. Treats the reader as a peer rather than a
student. Good if you think your audience needs the question framed
before they'll engage with the answer.

### Candidate D — Contrarian, confident

> "Europe bought its intelligence from California for a decade and
> called it a strategy."

Sharpest of the four. Confident authorial voice from sentence one.
Risk: sounds anti-American if the chapter doesn't immediately pivot
to the "we depend on each other" framing.

### Recommendation

**A or B.** A is safer and ages best; B has more voice. Avoid C (too
long) and D (too confrontational for paragraph one). Whichever you
pick, the second sentence should do the "and here's why that's
suddenly a problem" work.

## 7. Interview strategy

### Target count

Roughly 15–20 interviews across the book. Fewer means the book lacks
primary research; more means diminishing returns and scheduling drag.

### Lead time

C-level interviews are 4–8 weeks from first outreach to scheduled
call. Begin outreach now for chapters you plan to write in months
2–4. The writing schedule should be downstream of the interview
schedule, not the other way around.

### Tracking

A simple spreadsheet or Notion table:

### Outreach templates

#### Template A — Cold email (no warm introduction)

> Subject: *The Sovereign AI Company* — 30 minutes on [their specific
> topic]
>
> [Their name],
>
> I'm writing a book called *The Sovereign AI Company* — an
> operational playbook for European executives on how to hedge their
> AI infrastructure across the US-China bipolar model landscape. It's
> the follow-up to *AI Immigrants*. [Publisher or self-pub context
> if relevant.]
>
> Chapter [N] is about [specific topic]. Your work on [specific
> thing] is one of the most honest examples I've seen of [what they
> actually did]. I'd like to spend 30 minutes on a call hearing about
> it in your own words — on the record by default, on background if
> you prefer, and I'll send you the quotes I plan to use before the
> book goes to press.
>
> Would any time in the next two to three weeks work? Happy to fit
> around your calendar.
>
> Best,
> Kevin

Keep it short. Name one specific thing about their work — not
generic flattery. Offer background-mode explicitly; it's the only
way you'll get some of the most useful interviews.

#### Template B — Warm introduction

When someone is willing to introduce you, give them a forwardable
two-sentence ask:

> "Happy to introduce you. Could you send me a forwardable note?"
>
> "Absolutely — here it is:
>
> [Name] is writing *The Sovereign AI Company*, a successor to *AI
> Immigrants* aimed at European executives navigating AI
> infrastructure dependencies. He's doing a chapter on [topic] and
> would really value 30 minutes with you on [specific thing]. On
> background is fine. I think you'd find it a good conversation."

One email, easy to forward, specific enough that the recipient knows
why they'd take the call.

#### Template C — Follow-up (once)

> Subject: (same as original)
>
> [Name] — just bumping this in case the original went into the
> archive. Happy to leave it if now is not the right time. K.

Single follow-up, a week after the first email. If no response,
move on. Reading into silence is a waste of time.

### Question banks by interview type

*Design every interview to leave with three things: a usable quote,
a new named person to talk to, and a fact you didn't know before
the call started.*

#### For Chapter 4 — CTO who moved serious workload off hyperscaler

1. When did "off hyperscale" first become a real conversation
   internally? Who raised it?
2. What was the total-cost-of-ownership analysis that tipped the
   decision? What numbers actually moved the board?
3. What did you underestimate — technically, commercially, or
   politically?
4. Which workloads stayed on the hyperscaler, and why?
5. What does procurement look like now? How did the vendor
   relationships change?
6. If Taiwan went hot tomorrow, what would break in your stack?
7. What would you do differently if you started over?
8. Who else in Europe has done this well, in your view, that people
   don't know about?

#### For Chapter 7 — Head of AI running production on Mistral or open-weight

1. What's the split between frontier APIs and open-weight / Mistral in
   your actual production workload today?
2. Which tasks did you expect to need frontier reasoning for but
   ended up not needing?
3. Where does the quality gap bite — which workloads genuinely don't
   work on open-weight today?
4. What's your fine-tuning or distillation approach?
5. How do you evaluate? Is there a framework you can share?
6. Latency, cost, accuracy — which one is usually the binding
   constraint?
7. What would have to be true for you to push 80% of production to
   European or open-weight models?
8. Who else should I talk to who's further along than you are?

#### For Chapter 13 — Mistral / ASML / national lab / serious AI research figure

1. Do you think a "Linux of AI" outcome is actually achievable for
   Europe? What's the honest probability?
2. What would have to happen institutionally — who funds it, who
   leads it, what's the governance model?
3. What's the primary gap? Compute, talent, coordination, or
   political will?
4. Who's the plausible lead organisation? Can it be Mistral? Should
   it be?
5. What's the realistic timeline to a genuinely competitive
   open-weight European model?
6. What kills this? What are the failure modes that Gaia-X showed us?
7. What role should the UK play, post-Brexit?
8. What would change your mind?

#### For Chapter 16 — Executive representing one of the four postures

1. Why this posture for your company? Walk me through the decision
   structure.
2. What were the alternatives considered seriously, and why were
   they rejected?
3. What was the board's primary concern? How was it addressed?
4. What's the escape hatch if the posture turns out wrong?
5. What signal would make you change posture? What are you
   monitoring?
6. Who else in Europe is doing this posture well? Who is doing it
   badly?
7. What would you tell a peer who is about to adopt the same posture?

### Running the calls

- Record with permission, every time. Transcribe same-day.
- Send the usable quotes back for approval before the book goes to
  press. This alone converts a quarter of reluctant interviewees to
  yes.
- Ask for one introduction at the end of every call. Don't skip this
  step. Every interview is two or three more interviews.
- Publish the acknowledgements list. Interviewees become early
  reviewers, amplifiers, and — if you do it right — evangelists.
